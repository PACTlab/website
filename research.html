PACT Lab

Research

We study how cochlear pathologies shape hearing — and how to measure them precisely enough to change how we diagnose and treat hearing loss.

Overview

The prevalence and pathological complexity of sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) both increase with age, yet its impact on communication varies dramatically across individuals. Two patients with identical audiograms can have profoundly different hearing experiences. The audiogram reflects only one consequence of cochlear dysfunction — reduced audibility — while leaving the specific pathology invisible. Our research aims to change that by establishing physiological profiling as the basis for precision diagnosis of hearing loss.

Research themes

Physiological profiling of sensorineural hearing loss

Temporal bone histology shows SNHL can result from damage to outer and inner hair cells, atrophy of the stria vascularis, cochlear synaptopathy, and loss of auditory nerve fibers — often in combination. Each pathology disrupts suprathreshold sound coding independently of its effect on hearing thresholds. We develop and validate a multimetric battery of non-invasive biomarkers to detect and differentiate these pathologies, moving from a diagnosis centered on audibility toward one grounded in pathophysiology.

Improving diagnostic precision and measurement reliability

Realizing the potential of a biomarker battery requires understanding both what each measure is sensitive to (diagnostic precision) and how to reduce variability to improve reliability and efficiency (measurement precision). Our work includes investigations of how the medial olivocochlear efferent system influences OAE measures and studies of forward-pressure-level calibration to control for ear canal acoustic variability — with the goal of making precision diagnostics practical in real-world clinical settings.

Cross-species translational models

We use a synergistic cross-species framework to build mechanistic understanding that bridges basic science and clinical practice. Preclinical models — including chinchilla models of noise-induced and ototoxic hearing loss and nonhuman primate behavioral paradigms — allow histological verification of pathology and precise exposure control. Human studies ground our findings in clinical reality and ensure that the diagnostics we develop are implementable outside the research lab.

Current project

Physiological profiling of age-related and noise-induced SNHL  Active

This project validates a multimetric physiological biomarker battery against two divergent profiles of cochlear damage associated with aging and noise exposure. We recruit adults aged 45–75 into two cohorts: high sensory-risk (younger adults with significant noise exposure history) and high metabolic-risk (older adults with low noise exposure). Biomarkers of auditory nerve function, inner hair cell integrity, and outer hair cell function are measured to identify diagnostic profiles at the individual and group level. A second aim tests whether these profiles predict suprathreshold perception and self-reported hearing difficulty beyond what audibility alone can explain.
